The Cassava Paradox

Nigeria is the world's largest producer — but not its most productive.

Nigeria grows more cassava than any nation on earth. It also grows it less efficiently than Thailand, Vietnam, or Indonesia — countries that produce far less in volume, yet extract far more value per hectare. High output is not the same as high performance.

Data: FAO FAOSTAT 2022 · IITA · World Bank  |  Published 2026-05-24
§ 1 — The paradox, in three numbers

World's largest producer. Near-lowest yield.

In 2022 Nigeria harvested approximately 59.5 million tonnes of cassava — more than any other country, and roughly double the production of the second-largest producer. Yet on the single metric that determines long-run competitiveness — yield per hectare — Nigeria ranks among the lowest of all major producing nations. The gap is not marginal.

The yield potential gap: IITA field trials have consistently demonstrated that improved cassava varieties under good management can yield 25–40 tonnes/hectare in Nigerian conditions [2]. The gap between demonstrated potential (~30 t/ha) and national average (~8.2 t/ha) represents the maximum addressable improvement available — a roughly 3–4× multiplier without adding a single extra hectare.
§ 3 — What each country's land achieves

Nigeria plants five times more land to produce twice the volume

The land-use comparison makes the structural inefficiency concrete. Thailand produces roughly 53% of Nigeria's output from 21% of Nigeria's harvested area. Every hectare in Thailand delivers more than 2.5 times what a hectare in Nigeria delivers.

§ 4 — What happens after harvest

Traditional processing dominates; industrial processing remains limited

Volume is only part of the story. The post-harvest pathway determines how much economic value a tonne of cassava ultimately generates. Here the contrast between Nigeria and Thailand is sharpest.

IITA research on Nigeria's cassava system consistently documents that the large majority of production flows into traditional food processing — predominantly garri, fufu, and lafun — consumed domestically. Industrial processing into native starch, glucose syrup, and ethanol exists but is small relative to total production. Thailand, by contrast, has built an export-oriented industrial processing complex; virtually all Thai cassava is processed into starch, chips, or pellets for export markets [2][3].

On specific processing share figures: Precise percentage breakdowns of Nigerian cassava into industrial vs. traditional channels are not published as a single audited annual figure. IITA characterises industrial processing as "a small fraction" of total production [2]. The World Bank's 2020 Nigeria Agricultural Sector Risk Assessment notes that cassava value chains are "predominantly subsistence and semi-commercial" [4]. Exact splits vary by source and year; only directional characterisation is used here.

§ 5 — Value chain pathways

The further from the farm gate, the more value is created — or lost

Cassava's fundamental economic property is that it is a low-value bulk commodity at harvest and a progressively higher-value product as it is processed. Nigeria's value chain is largely truncated: most cassava travels one or two steps. Thailand's chain runs to export-grade modified starch and bioethanol — products with substantially higher market prices per tonne of raw material equivalent.

§ 6 — Productivity economics

Growth from land, not from learning

Total Factor Productivity (TFP) measures how much output an agricultural system extracts from a given bundle of inputs — land, labour, capital, and materials combined. When TFP grows, farmers produce more with the same inputs. When it stagnates, more output requires proportionally more inputs.

The World Bank and USDA Economic Research Service publish agricultural TFP indices for major producing countries. The structural finding for Nigeria — consistent across datasets — is that output growth in Nigerian agriculture, including cassava, has been driven predominantly by input expansion (more land, more labour) rather than productivity gains. Thailand's cassava sector, by contrast, achieved yield growth through variety improvement and post-harvest investment [4][6].

On TFP data specificity: The USDA ERS International Agricultural Productivity dataset provides country-level TFP indices. Country-specific cassava TFP is not separately published. The characterisation here — that Nigeria's cassava growth is extensification-led rather than intensification-led — draws on IITA agronomic research [2] and the World Bank's Nigeria agricultural risk assessment [4], not a single cassava-specific TFP series. That series does not exist in the public literature to the precision required for a chart.

§ 7 — The arithmetic of closing the gap

What Nigeria could produce without planting one more hectare

The yield gap calculation is straightforward arithmetic from published FAO data, combined with IITA's documented achievable on-farm yields. If Nigeria's 7.27 million harvested hectares achieved the yield Thailand achieves today (20.8 t/ha), national production would be approximately 151 million tonnes — more than 2.5 times current output — from the same land.

If the more conservative target of IITA-demonstrated smallholder yields under improved management (~15 t/ha, well below experiment-station maxima) were achieved nationally, production would reach approximately 109 million tonnes — still an 83% increase [2][1].
