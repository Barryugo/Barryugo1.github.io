Nigeria and Ghana — two neighbours, two electricity realities.

Nigeria has the largest installed power capacity in sub-Saharan Africa. Ghana has a much smaller fleet — yet delivers more electricity per person, loses less to its grid, and connects more of its citizens to power. The pages below show the data, with sources, and nothing else.

§ 1 — The 2024 ledger

What each country's grid looks like today

Nigeria's nameplate capacity is roughly 13 GW. On most days, less than 5 GW actually reaches the meter — a ratio that encapsulates the country's infrastructure challenge. Ghana's smaller fleet of 5.5 GW operates with far greater reliability and reaches 85% of its population, versus Nigeria's 57%.

§ 2 — Electricity access

Who has power — and who does not

Ghana's National Electrification Scheme (NES) has connected an additional 13 percentage points of the population since 2015, reaching 85% by 2024. Nigeria's access rate has improved more slowly — from roughly 45% in 2015 to 57% — a consequence of population growth outpacing grid expansion.

§ 3 — Installed capacity, by source

What is on the grid — and what generates it

Nigeria's generation fleet is dominated by natural gas (>80% of capacity), with hydropower (~2 GW) as the main non-fossil source. Coal is negligible. Ghana's mix is similarly gas-heavy but with a proportionally larger hydro base — the Akosombo Dam alone provides ~1 GW — and a small but growing solar fleet.

Ghana's utilisation is closer to 70%, though drought years reduce Akosombo's output significantly.
§ 4 — Electricity generation

Total output and what each person receives

Nigeria generated approximately 30 TWh in 2024 — a figure that has barely moved since 2010. Ghana generated around 18 TWh, from a much smaller base. On a per-capita basis, with Nigeria's 223 million people versus Ghana's 34 million, the gap inverts dramatically.

§ 5 — Transmission & distribution losses

How much power disappears before it reaches the meter

System losses — theft, leakage, ageing infrastructure, and metering failure — are the silent tax on every unit generated. Nigeria's losses run at roughly 45% of generation; Ghana's at around 22%. For context: the global average for developing economies is ~15%; the OECD average is ~6%.

§ 6 — Renewables

Solar and hydro: the two levers both countries hold

Both countries sit in a high-irradiance equatorial belt — daily solar potential exceeds 5 kWh/m² across most of each country. Both have significant untapped hydro resources. Yet solar capacity remains below 0.5 GW in each. The contrast here is with aspiration: Nigeria's Renewable Energy Master Plan targets 29 GW of renewable capacity by 2030; Ghana's renewable targets aim for 10% of electricity from non-hydro renewables by 2030.

Ghana's line is dashed to distinguish from Nigeria's (both converge at ~0.5 GW). Sources: IRENA Renewable Capacity Statistics 2025 [5]. Off-grid solar (households, telecom towers) is excluded.

The off-grid asterisk: Official grid-connected solar figures understate actual solar deployment in both countries. Off-grid and rooftop systems — particularly in Nigeria, where generator-dependent households install solar as a substitute — may add several hundred megawatts to each country's effective solar capacity.

§ 8 — What it means

A structural gap, not just a scale gap

The conventional reading is that Nigeria's grid underperforms because the country is too large, too poor, or too complex. The data complicates this. Nigeria's installed capacity per person (58 W) is already below Ghana's (162 W), but even that capacity largely sits idle. The problem is not only one of investment volume — it is system integration: gas supply reliability, transmission infrastructure, distribution network maintenance, metering, and the commercial viability of electricity companies.

Ghana's path has not been painless. Dumsor — the load-shedding cycles that plagued the country from 2012 to 2016 — demonstrated what fiscal constraints on generation capacity can do. Ghana stabilised partly through emergency thermal capacity additions and a harder negotiating position with independent power producers.

Both countries sit below any reasonable threshold for economic electrification. The global benchmark for productive electricity use is typically cited at 1,000–2,000 kWh per person per year. Nigeria is at 135; Ghana at 530. The gap between them is meaningful — but both are a long way from where manufacturing-led economic development begins.
